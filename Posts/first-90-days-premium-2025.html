Understanding Why the First 90 Days Matter

Most leaders dramatically underestimate the importance of their first 90 days in a new role. They assume they can take time to settle in, understand the situation, and implement changes gradually. Meanwhile, their teams, peers, and broader organization are already forming impressions, making assumptions about the new leader's competence and intentions. Early missteps or unclear direction are interpreted through negative lenses. Ambiguous actions are assumed to be problematic. Watkins' research demonstrates that the leaders most successful in new roles don't try to figure things out privately before acting. Instead, they establish presence and credibility quickly while maintaining appropriate humility about the learning required. They make visible progress on key issues while investing heavily in understanding organizational dynamics. They build alliances and begin establishing the foundations for transformation, all while still in the critical credibility-building phase.

The stakes are highest during this period because people's tolerance for learning and adjustment is highest when you're new. Months down the road, people expect you to understand how things work and act decisively. In the first 90 days, they're more forgiving of the learning process. This creates an unusual window where you can ask questions, observe, assess, and implement changes with more flexibility than you'll have later. Watkins reveals that success requires exploiting this window strategically while building the credibility and momentum necessary to drive lasting change.

The Core Framework for the First 90 Days

Watkins presents a phased approach to the first 90 days. Phase 1 (the first 30 days) focuses on establishing presence and credibility while learning deeply about the situation. You're assessing the organizational culture, understanding key relationships and politics, learning about your team and their capabilities, and identifying the most critical challenges and opportunities. Rather than immediately implementing changes, you're gathering intelligence while demonstrating your leadership presence through listening, asking thoughtful questions, and providing initial direction on how you'll approach the transition.

Phase 2 (days 30-60) focuses on deepening understanding and building alliances. You've learned the basic landscape; now you're diving deeper into specific challenges, understanding the politics more completely, and strategically building relationships with key stakeholders. You're identifying which battles matter most and beginning to shape direction while still in learning mode. You're signaling what kind of leader you are while maintaining enough mystery and openness to avoid premature judgments.

Phase 3 (days 60-90) focuses on building momentum toward transformation. You've learned, built alliances, and signaled direction. Now you're beginning to implement changes that demonstrate progress and establish momentum. You're making visible progress on key issues while building the foundation for longer-term transformation. You're establishing the rhythms, processes, and team dynamics that will enable sustained success.

The Specific Strategies for Success

Within this phased framework, Watkins provides specific guidance on critical leadership behaviors and decisions. First, establish a "learning agenda"‚Äîclear understanding of what you need to learn in the early weeks and months. Rather than random exploration, you're systematically gathering intelligence about the most critical issues. Second, build alliances strategically. Certain relationships are disproportionately important; investing in these relationships early establishes support for the changes you'll need to drive. Third, make early wins visible. You need to demonstrate that you can accomplish things, that you understand how to get things done in this organization, and that you're driving progress, not just studying the situation.

Fourth, identify the core challenges that require transformation. Not everything requires changing, but certain critical issues demand attention. Identifying these correctly and communicating why they matter builds support for change. Fifth, secure early wins on high-leverage issues. These wins build momentum, establish your leadership credibility, and fund the political capital necessary for larger transformations. Sixth, establish team dynamics and engagement. Your team's confidence in your leadership and their clarity about direction are critical for everything that follows.
