Why Most Ideas Fail to Stick

Every day you're exposed to thousands of attempted messages: advertisements, news stories, advice from friends, organizational communications, marketing campaigns. Yet you remember virtually none of them. Not because you lack capacity but because most messages lack the structural characteristics that enable memory formation and retention. Ideas fail not from inadequate importance but from poor design. A well-designed message on a trivial topic sticks better than a poorly designed message on vital content.

The Six Principles That Make Ideas Stick (SUCCESs)

At the heart of "Made to Stick" lies the acronym SUCCESs, representing six principles that reliably make ideas memorable. Simplicity requires distilling messages to their essential core. Most communicators attempt conveying comprehensive complexity, overwhelming audiences with detail. Sticky messages instead focus on the single most important idea and protect it fiercely from diluting details. A nuclear safety message doesn't attempt educating about radiation physics; it emphasizes the single idea that following procedures keeps you safe.

Unexpectedness captures attention by surprising audiences and violating their expectations. Predictable messages fade into background noise; unexpected messages demand attention and create memory encoding strength. This doesn't require shock value or sensationalism; genuine unexpectedness‚Äîthe surprising consequence or counterintuitive insight‚Äîcreates the attentional capture necessary for message retention.

Concreteness enables people to visualize and understand ideas through specific, tangible examples rather than abstract generalizations. "Good financial planning" proves forgettable; "Save your first dollar every day" creates concrete, visualizable, actionable guidance. Concrete examples create mental imagery and emotional engagement far more effectively than abstract concepts.

Credibility establishes authority and trust, necessary for message acceptance. This might come from expertise, statistical evidence, antiheroes who've experienced the problem, or other sources that signal legitimacy. Messages without credibility foundations struggle for acceptance regardless of logical merit. Sticky ideas establish credibility through multiple channels, not relying on single sources.

Emotion creates motivation for audience action. Abstract arguments without emotional grounding fail to move people to behavior change. Effective sticky ideas trigger emotional responses‚Äînot through manipulation but through genuine emotional relevance. Making audiences feel something‚Äîconcern, hope, inspiration, determination‚Äîcreates the motivation necessary for message acceptance and action.

Story provides the narrative structure and context within which ideas become memorable and applicable. Stories enable audiences to envision how ideas apply in real situations, create emotional engagement, and provide memory structures far superior to facts alone. Humans think naturally in narratives; ideas framed within compelling stories adhere far more effectively than standalone propositions.

Practical Communication Strategies

Implementation begins with message simplification. Before crafting your message, identify the single most important idea you need audiences to understand and remember. Resist comprehensive completeness; focus on core concept. A healthcare organization attempting to communicate about healthy lifestyle change doesn't enumerate exercise routines, nutritional details, and sleep science. Instead, it simplifies to a core idea: "Small consistent steps create lasting health change." This simplicity doesn't sacrifice truth; it clarifies priority.

Introduce unexpectedness through genuine surprise. Rather than shocking for shock value, violate audience expectations about how your topic typically appears. If everyone assumes your company prioritizes profit, unexpected revelation that you prioritize environmental responsibility creates attention. If everyone knows health advice is boring, unexpected storytelling approach creates engagement. This unexpectedness doesn't require controversy; genuine novelty suffices.

Develop concrete, specific examples replacing abstract concepts. Rather than discussing "innovation," describe a specific team member implementing a novel process that dramatically improved efficiency. Rather than "climate change urgency," describe specific communities experiencing specific consequences. Concrete specificity creates mental imagery, emotional resonance, and memory formation far exceeding abstract discussion.

Creating Stick-Worthy Messages

Begin by evaluating whether your current messages achieve stickiness. Ask audiences to recall your key messages days or weeks later. Can they articulate your core idea? If not, your message lacks stickiness despite potential importance. Apply the SUCCESs framework: Is your message sufficiently simple? Does it include unexpected elements? Can you visualize specific examples? Have you established credibility? Does it trigger emotion? Is there narrative structure?

Systematically redesign messages applying each principle. What would happen if you simplified to single core idea? How might you add unexpectedness without sensationalism? What concrete examples illuminate abstract concepts? What credibility sources support your claims? How can you create emotional resonance? What story frames your message within compelling narrative? Each principle offers leverage point for message improvement.

Test messages with small audiences before broad deployment. Can they recall your message later? Do they understand your core idea? Does the message move them to action? Feedback reveals which principles you've applied effectively and which require strengthening. This iterative refinement produces increasingly sticky messages.
