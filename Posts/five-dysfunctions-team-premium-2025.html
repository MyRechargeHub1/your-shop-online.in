The Five Dysfunctions That Cripple Teams

The foundation of team dysfunction is Absence of Trustâ€”the unwillingness to make oneself vulnerable to teammates. When team members don't trust each other, they protect themselves, hide mistakes, downplay struggles, and position themselves as infallible. This consumes enormous energy maintaining a facade and prevents the honest conversations necessary for genuine collaboration. Trust emerges paradoxically through vulnerabilityâ€”when leaders and team members acknowledge their limitations, admit struggles, and ask for help, it creates psychological safety that enables others to do the same.

Fear of Conflict emerges directly from lack of trust. Teams with insufficient trust avoid the healthy debate necessary for sound decision-making. Because people fear that conflict will damage relationships, they suppress disagreement, leading to artificial consensus on important decisions. The team makes decisions without benefit of diverse perspectives, missing critical considerations. Ironically, absence of conflict increases conflict significantlyâ€”people make decisions that others secretly oppose, leading to passive resistance and undermining behavior.

Lack of Commitment follows naturally. When people haven't debated options thoroughly, they never truly commit to decisions. Even if they verbally agree, they maintain private doubts, reducing their emotional investment in success. This lack of true commitment manifests as low accountabilityâ€”people don't fully own their work because they never genuinely committed to the direction.

Avoidance of Accountability represents the fourth dysfunction. Without genuine commitment, holding people accountable feels like personal attack rather than mutual expectation. This dysfunction is particularly damaging because it tolerates mediocre performance and avoids the difficult conversations necessary to maintain standards. High performers become demoralized when they observe lower-performing team members facing no consequences.

Inattention to Collective Resultsâ€”the final dysfunctionâ€”emerges when team members prioritize individual achievement over team success. People focus on their department's success, their project's outcome, or their personal advancement rather than the team's shared goals. This last dysfunction undermines the foundation: without shared commitment to collective results, none of the previous issues matter because people aren't aligned around what success actually looks like.

How Leaders Address These Dysfunctions

Building trust requires deliberate leadership. Lencioni emphasizes that leaders must model vulnerability firstâ€”acknowledging weaknesses, admitting mistakes, asking for input. Team members will only become vulnerable when they see their leader doing it safely. Trust-building activities, while useful, prove insufficient without the fundamental shift in psychological safety that comes from genuine leader vulnerability. Once trust exists, leaders can create forums for healthy conflict where disagreement is encouraged but disrespect isn't tolerated. This requires skilled facilitation and clear norms about the difference between arguing about ideas and attacking people.

Commitment emerges through inclusive decision-making where diverse perspectives are genuinely solicited and considered. Rather than leaders making decisions unilaterally, effective teams ensure input before deciding, then move forward with full commitment even if not everyone's preference was chosen. Accountability becomes possible through clear expectations, progress tracking, and direct conversation about shortfalls. High performance is maintained through mutual accountability among peers, not just hierarchical accountability from leaders.
