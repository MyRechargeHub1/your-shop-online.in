The Three Rules That Drive Epidemics

Gladwell identifies the Law of the Fewâ€”the principle that epidemics are driven by a small number of exceptional people who possess unusual social networks or communication gifts. Some people are "connectors," with vast networks spanning diverse social groups. Others are "mavens," with expertise and credibility that makes others trust their recommendations. Still others are "salesmen," with gifts for persuasion and charisma. Epidemics require these exceptional individuals as catalysts. Understanding this law enables change agents to identify and mobilize the key connectors, mavens, and salesmen who can drive epidemics.

The Stickiness Factor addresses why some messages stick while others fade. A sticky message creates disproportionate impact despite containing the same information as non-sticky messages. Gladwell provides examples of how minor changes in message format, presentation, or context dramatically increase stickiness. A television program redesigned its approach based on stickiness research and dramatically increased educational outcomes. A public health campaign modified its message and achieved better results with lower budgets. Understanding stickiness principles enables creators to craft messages that actually stick with audiences.

The Power of Context establishes that the same person may behave completely differently in different contexts, and small environmental changes can dramatically alter behavior. The broken windows theory illustrates this principleâ€”neighborhoods where minor vandalism is addressed promptly show less serious crime, while neighborhoods where minor vandalism is ignored descend toward greater criminal activity. Context shapes behavior powerfully, meaning that creating epidemics sometimes involves changing context rather than persuading individuals directly.
