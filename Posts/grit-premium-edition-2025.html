Understanding Grit: Passion Plus Perseverance

Duckworth defines grit as combining two essential components: passion and perseverance. The passion component proves particularly important. Duckworth's research reveals that genuine passion typically develops through extended engagement with activities. The Psychology of Why Grit Predicts Achievement

Several psychological mechanisms explain why grit predicts achievement more reliably than talent. First, grit correlates with effort expenditure. Those with high grit invest substantially more hours in deliberate practice, skill development, and goal-pursuing activities. Over time, this additional practice accumulates into genuine expertise. Someone with moderate natural talent who practices daily will eventually exceed someone with greater natural talent who practices occasionally; sustained effort compounds. Duckworth calls this the "effort multiplier"â€”the reality that sustained effort over extended periods produces achievement that occasional intense effort cannot match.

Second, grit correlates with effective goal setting. Those with high perseverance tend to set demanding but achievable goals, maintain focus on those goals despite distractions, and strategically revise goals as circumstances change while maintaining ultimate direction. Rather than setting vague aspirations, they establish specific targets and track progress, enabling them to recognize whether approaches are working. Third, grit correlates with resilience and effective coping with setback. When initial approaches fail, those with high grit analyze what didn't work, identify improved approaches, and persist in pursuit of goals. Those with lower grit tend to interpret setbacks as evidence of permanent inadequacy and abandon pursuits.
